  • Info

    • 1. The signing of the Treaty of Waitangi in 1840 by the Māori chiefs and the British Crown is considered a turning point in South Island history. The treaty's relevance extends beyond its historical setting as it aimed to provide a framework for respectful and peaceful coexistence.

    2. Disparities between the Māori and English versions, however, planted the seeds of distrust and struggles, resulting in land conflicts and the fall of Māori culture.

    3. As Māori rights were violated and lands were wrongfully taken, anger grew over time. The cooperation and protection tenets of the treaty continue to be essential to New Zealand's legal framework and cultural norms but in spite of these obstacles.

    The Waitangi Tribunal's formation demonstrates the country's dedication to adressing historical wrongs and promoting peace.

    4. Every year on February 6th, Waitangi Day is marked with tenderness as a reminder of the continuous struggle towards reconciliation and unity.

    5. It's an opportunity for introspection, discussion, and reaffirmed dedication to preserving the treaty's tenets of respect, understanding, and collaboration between Māori and non-Māori communities.

    6. The Treaty of Waitangi serves as a symbol of hope and togehterness for future generations as New Zealand navigates its common history and shapes its relationship with each other.
